# Cross-Client Pattern Library

> What works across clients gets logged here. Patterns must be validated by data — not opinions or gut feel. This is how the system compounds intelligence over time.
>
> Owner: Growth Strategist | Updated: Quarterly minimum, or whenever a pattern is confirmed across 1+ client contexts

## How to Use

1. When an experiment wins or a tactic works exceptionally well, ask: is this client-specific or universal?
2. If universal (would work for other products/audiences), log it here using the format below
3. Before planning any new client's strategy, scan this library for applicable patterns
4. Patterns are tagged by growth stage for fast lookup: Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue
5. Never add a pattern based on opinion. Evidence is required — minimum one validated result with a metric

## Rules for Adding Patterns

- Must include a real metric (CPA, CVR, open rate, lift %, etc.) — no "it felt like it worked"
- Must include context so future users can judge applicability (ICP type, channel, product type)
- Must include a mechanism — the psychological or structural reason it works
- Must include When to skip — knowing where a pattern breaks is as valuable as knowing where it works
- Tag with growth stage. If it spans multiple stages, log under primary stage and note the secondary

## Pattern Entry Format

```
PATTERN ENTRY
─────────────
Pattern:      [one-line description]
Stage:        [Acquisition | Activation | Retention | Referral | Revenue]
Evidence:     [metrics that proved it]
Context:      [ICP type, product type, channel]
Mechanism:    [why it works — the psychology or structural reason]
When to use:  [conditions where this applies]
When to skip: [conditions where this doesn't apply]
Source:       [client/campaign, date]
```

## Acquisition Patterns

```
PATTERN ENTRY
─────────────
Pattern:      Curiosity (variable reward) beats pain messaging for latent-pain ICPs
Stage:        Acquisition
Evidence:     $2.36 CPA (curiosity angle) vs $8.86 CPA (pain angle) — 73% lower CPA.
              2.35% CTR on curiosity creative vs 0.9% on pain. Meta ads, cold audience.
Context:      Consumer app, casual users who don't actively feel the problem day-to-day.
              Low-awareness audience with untapped value they haven't discovered yet.
              Channel: Meta paid social, cold audience targeting.
Mechanism:    Latent-pain audiences haven't tried to solve the problem — so pain messaging
              lands as irrelevant. They don't feel the pain yet. Curiosity ("what could this
              be worth?") triggers variable reward anticipation, the same loop as a slot
              machine or a scratch ticket. The brain wants to resolve the open loop.
When to use:  Any product where users have untapped value they haven't consciously quantified.
              Low-awareness audiences. Consumer apps with a discovery/reveal mechanic.
              Collectibles, finance tools, audit-style products.
When to skip: Active-pain audiences who are already searching for solutions — they want
              confirmation, not curiosity. B2B where the pain is explicit and quantified.
              Retargeting audiences who already know the product.
Source:       Consumer app campaign (product valuation/discovery app), March 2026
```

```
PATTERN ENTRY
─────────────
Pattern:      Nostalgia + curiosity blend outperforms pure nostalgia for consumer products
Stage:        Acquisition
Evidence:     Pure nostalgia creative: $2.77 CPA. Curiosity creative: $2.36 CPA.
              Nostalgia was strong but curiosity was stronger. Blend hypothesis (V6) queued
              for next test phase based on mechanism analysis.
Context:      Consumer products with emotional/nostalgic connection. Hobby and collection
              verticals. Casual audiences who grew up with the product category.
              Channel: Meta paid social, cold audience.
Mechanism:    Pure nostalgia creates a warm emotional state — but warm emotions don't
              automatically trigger clicks. Adding a curiosity hook converts the emotion
              into forward action. "Those cards you loved as a kid grew up too — what are
              they worth now?" The nostalgia opens the emotional door; the curiosity
              question gives a reason to walk through it.
When to use:  Products with a childhood or heritage connection. Anything in hobby,
              collection, or legacy verticals. Audiences with a shared cultural reference
              point around the product category.
When to skip: B2B contexts where emotional heritage isn't a purchase driver. Products with
              no emotional history or cultural resonance. Audiences too young to have
              nostalgia for the category.
Source:       Consumer app campaign (product valuation/discovery app), March 2026
```

## Activation Patterns

```
PATTERN ENTRY
─────────────
Pattern:      Onboarding email within 1 hour of signup drives outsized first-action rate
Stage:        Activation
Evidence:     71% first-scan rate within 24 hours of signup. 67% email open rate on
              the immediate post-signup trigger. Baseline industry open rate: ~25–30%.
Context:      Consumer app with a single, clear "aha moment" action (scan a card).
              First action takes under 2 minutes. Email triggered by signup event,
              sent within 60 minutes. No drip delay.
Mechanism:    Post-signup momentum is highest immediately after the decision to register.
              The user is most motivated, most curious, and least distracted at T+0.
              Every hour of delay bleeds motivation. Immediate email with a single,
              specific CTA captures that momentum before life interrupts. Multiple CTAs
              kill it — one action only.
When to use:  Any product with a clear, singular "aha moment" action. Works best when
              the first action is simple and completable in under 2 minutes. Consumer apps,
              tools with immediate value output, audit/scan/estimate mechanics.
When to skip: Complex products where the first value requires multi-step setup or
              onboarding before the user can experience anything. If the product isn't
              ready to deliver value in the first session, a fast email creates false
              urgency and increases churn.
Source:       Consumer app campaign (product valuation/discovery app), March 2026
```
