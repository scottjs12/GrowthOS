> These are starting frameworks, not rigid scripts. Adapt section order to the product and audience. Never fill a template mechanically — every section must earn its place.

## STRUCTURE 1: Problem-Agitate-Solve (PAS)

**Best for:** Active-pain audiences, B2B, high-consideration products, long sales cycles
**Section count:** 5 core sections
**When to use:** Audience already knows they have the problem. Pain is conscious and felt. Product is the solution to a named frustration.
**When to avoid:** Low-awareness audiences who don't feel the pain yet — agitating a problem they haven't named will confuse them, not convert them.

**Hero formula:**
- Headline: State the problem plainly. No clever wordplay. "[The thing that's costing you X]"
- Subhead: Agitate — what it's costing them in real terms (time, money, opportunity)
- CTA: Introduce the solution as relief. "Fix it in [timeframe]" or "End [problem] today"

**Section structure:**
1. **Hero** — Problem headline + agitate subhead + primary CTA
2. **Agitate deeper** — "Here's what happens if you don't fix this." Quantify the cost of inaction. 2–3 pain points, specific and visceral.
3. **Solution** — What the product does. Lead with outcomes, not features. "You get X so you can Y." Features serve benefits — never list features standalone.
4. **Proof** — Testimonials with specific results, case studies, logos. Proof must match the pain stated in section 2 — if you agitated churn, your testimonials must reference churn solved.
5. **CTA** — Repeat the transformation promise. "[From pain state] to [gain state]. Start today."

**Rules:**
- Never skip the agitate section — without it, the solution feels unmotivated
- Proof must map to the pain you agitated, not generic praise
- CTA should echo the headline transformation, not introduce new language

## STRUCTURE 2: Value-First (Show Don't Tell)

**Best for:** Tools, SaaS, products with visual or demonstrable output, design-forward brands
**Section count:** 5 core sections
**When to use:** The product's output is the proof. Showing the result is more compelling than describing the pain. Use when the demo or screenshot is your best salesperson.
**When to avoid:** Abstract or consulting-type products where the output is hard to visualize. Emotional/identity products where showing the output undersells the feeling.

**Hero formula:**
- Headline: State the benefit of the output. What does the result give the user?
- Subhead: Who it's for and how fast they get to the result
- CTA: "See it in action" / "Try it free" / "Get your [output] now"
- Visual: Screenshot, demo GIF, or output example. Above the fold. This is the hero.

**Section structure:**
1. **Hero** — Output visual + benefit headline + subhead + CTA
2. **How it works** — 3 numbered steps with visuals. Short labels, one-sentence explanations. Emphasize speed and simplicity.
3. **Features grid** — 4–6 features. Icon + bold label + one-liner benefit statement each. No paragraphs.
4. **Social proof** — Testimonials focused on outcomes ("I saved X hours / increased X by Y%"), not feelings ("I love this product"). Outcome-specific beats sentiment every time.
5. **Pricing or CTA block** — Price anchoring if applicable. Final CTA with risk-reducer ("No credit card required" / "Cancel anytime").

**Rules:**
- The visual in the hero must be self-explanatory — if you have to caption it, simplify it
- How-it-works steps must be 3 — not 4, not 5. More steps signal complexity
- Never put a testimonial above proof of what the product actually does

## STRUCTURE 3: Social Proof Heavy

**Best for:** Crowded markets, skeptical audiences, B2C consumer products, categories with trust deficit
**Section count:** 5 core sections
**When to use:** Audience has been burned before by similar products. High competition means differentiation is hard — trust becomes the differentiator. Works when you have meaningful scale (user count, recognizable customers).
**When to avoid:** Early-stage products with no meaningful proof yet. Don't fake volume — audiences read it immediately.

**Hero formula:**
- Headline: Lead with the number or the name. "50,000 teams trust [Product]" or a pull-quote from a recognizable customer
- Subhead: What they trust it for — the core use case
- CTA: "Join them" / "See why they switched"

**Section structure:**
1. **Hero** — Big social proof stat or bold testimonial headline + subhead + CTA
2. **Trust bar** — Logo bar (recognizable customers) + user count or review aggregate (stars + number). Visual credibility at a glance.
3. **Customer stories** — 3 stories, each 2–3 sentences: [Who they are] + [problem they had] + [specific result]. Not quotes. Narrative. "Acme went from X to Y in Z weeks."
4. **Feature highlights** — Brief. Proof-heavy page doesn't need feature depth — just enough to confirm capability. 3–4 bullets, outcome-framed.
5. **Final proof + CTA** — Repeat the scale number or aggregate rating. CTA echoes the trust theme: "See why [X] teams switched" / "Read all [N] reviews."

**Rules:**
- Every testimonial must include a name, company, and role — anonymized quotes kill credibility
- Customer story results must be specific — "increased revenue" is worthless, "increased MRR 40% in 6 weeks" is proof
- Never manufacture proof — misrepresentation destroys trust permanently

## STRUCTURE 4: Curiosity/Discovery

**Best for:** Low-awareness audiences, products with hidden or unrealized value, consumer apps, reveal/audit mechanics
**Section count:** 5 core sections
**When to use:** Audience doesn't know they have a problem or doesn't know what the answer might be. The product reveals something — a value, a score, a result. Curiosity is the acquisition hook.
**When to avoid:** Active-pain audiences who already know what they need — curiosity wastes their time. High-consideration B2B where buyers need credibility, not mystery.

**Hero formula:**
- Headline: Open a loop. Question or revelation format. "You might be sitting on $500 worth of..." / "Most [audience] don't know what their [thing] is actually worth."
- Subhead: Make the discovery feel low-effort and immediate. "Find out in 60 seconds."
- CTA: Action that resolves the open loop. "Discover / Scan / Check / Find out"

**Section structure:**
1. **Hero** — Curiosity headline (open loop) + low-effort subhead + discovery CTA
2. **How it works** — 3 steps, numbered. Must feel frictionless. "Snap a photo. We scan it. Get your result." No jargon, no explanation of the algorithm.
3. **Discovery examples** — "Users found..." — specific, concrete examples of what the product revealed. Real numbers make it credible. Real variety makes it feel personal.
4. **Lightweight social proof** — User count, app store rating, one or two punchy testimonials. Doesn't need to be heavy — curiosity already got them here.
5. **CTA** — Repeat the curiosity hook. Remind them the loop is still open. "Still wondering? Find out now."

**Rules:**
- Never resolve the curiosity in the copy — the product resolves it, not the page
- The "how it works" section must make the first action feel trivially easy
- Discovery examples must be specific enough to feel real, varied enough to feel personal

## STRUCTURE 5: Comparison/Alternative

**Best for:** Competitive markets, "switching" audiences, branded search traffic, SEO landing pages
**Section count:** 5 core sections
**When to use:** Audience is actively evaluating options. They're comparing you to a named competitor or searching for "[Competitor] alternative." Buyer intent is high — they just need a reason to pick you.
**When to avoid:** Top-of-funnel cold audiences who don't know the competitor. Bringing up a competitor introduces it to audiences who've never considered it.

**Hero formula:**
- Headline: Name the comparison directly. "[Product] vs. [Competitor]: [Key differentiator]" or "The [Competitor] alternative built for [use case]"
- Subhead: State your single biggest differentiator in plain language. Not "better" — specific. "Half the price with twice the integrations."
- CTA: Lower the switching friction. "Try free for 14 days" / "Migrate in 5 minutes"

**Section structure:**
1. **Hero** — Comparison headline + specific differentiator subhead + low-friction CTA
2. **Comparison table** — Feature-by-feature. Be honest — if they beat you on something, omit it rather than lie. Highlight your wins. Highlight their critical weaknesses. Keep it scannable.
3. **Differentiators (deep)** — Pick 2–3 things you genuinely win on and go deep. Don't skim 10 things — own 2–3 completely. Give the buyer something to remember.
4. **Switching story** — Make migration feel painless. "Import everything in one click." Address the #1 objection to switching ("But I'll lose all my data / history / settings") directly and specifically.
5. **CTA** — Echo the comparison frame. "Switch in 5 minutes" / "Start free — no [competitor's friction point] required."

**Rules:**
- Never be dishonest in the comparison table — sophisticated buyers will fact-check it and lose trust entirely
- Never attack the competitor personally — compare features, not character
- The switching section must address the real objection, not a strawman version of it
- This page type has high purchase intent — remove every possible friction from the CTA
